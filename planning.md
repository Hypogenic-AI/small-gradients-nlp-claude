# Research Plan: Small Gradients as a Mechanism for Synthetic Data Issues

## Motivation & Novelty Assessment

### Why This Research Matters
Synthetic data is increasingly used to train and fine-tune LLMs, yet two persistent problems lack mechanistic explanation: (a) synthetic data is less sample-efficient than human data, and (b) recursive training on synthetic data causes model collapse. Understanding the gradient-level mechanism connecting low perplexity to these outcomes would enable principled mitigation strategies rather than ad-hoc fixes.

### Gap in Existing Work
- Shumailov et al. (2023) noted that synthetic data produces "very small gradients" but did not systematically study this.
- Lin et al. (2025) measured weight update norms (ΔW) showing synthetic data produces ~3x smaller updates, but did not analyze per-step gradients or gradient *directions*.
- Dohmatob et al. (2024) explained collapse through tail truncation in data distributions, but the optimization-level mechanism (how tail loss maps to gradient dynamics) is unexplored.
- **No paper has decomposed gradients by direction** to test whether synthetic data over-represents gradients in "LLM-style" directions while under-representing gradients in tail/diverse directions.

### Our Novel Contribution
We provide the first systematic empirical study connecting:
1. Token-level perplexity differences (synthetic vs human) →
2. Per-step gradient magnitudes and directions →
3. Learning efficiency and collapse dynamics

Specifically, we test whether synthetic data's low perplexity causes gradient signals to be dominated by already-learned directions, starving the model of learning signal for distributional tails.

### Experiment Justification
- **Exp 1 (Perplexity)**: Establishes the baseline observation that synthetic data has lower perplexity. Required to confirm the premise.
- **Exp 2 (Gradient Norms)**: Tests the direct causal link: lower perplexity → smaller gradients. Core of the hypothesis.
- **Exp 3 (Gradient Directions)**: Tests the directional hypothesis: synthetic data gradients are concentrated in fewer directions, while human data gradients span a broader subspace. This is the key novel contribution.
- **Exp 4 (Learning Efficiency)**: Tests the practical consequence: more synthetic data needed for same learning, quantifying the relationship.

## Research Question
Does the low perplexity of LLM-generated synthetic data cause systematically smaller and directionally narrower gradients during fine-tuning, and does this explain both reduced sample efficiency and model collapse?

## Hypothesis Decomposition

**H1**: Synthetic data generated by an LLM has significantly lower perplexity (as measured by the same LLM) compared to human-authored data.

**H2**: Training on synthetic data produces smaller per-step gradient norms compared to training on human data.

**H3**: Gradients from synthetic data are more concentrated in a low-dimensional subspace (fewer principal components explain more variance), while human data gradients span a broader subspace.

**H4**: Achieving the same loss reduction requires more synthetic data samples than human data samples (lower sample efficiency).

## Proposed Methodology

### Approach
Use GPT-2 Small (124M params) as our base model — small enough for tractable gradient computation, large enough to exhibit meaningful language modeling behavior. Generate synthetic data by prompting the model to continue WikiText-2 prefixes, then compare gradient dynamics when fine-tuning on human vs. synthetic data.

### Experimental Steps

1. **Data Preparation**
   - Load WikiText-2 (train/val/test splits)
   - Generate synthetic WikiText-2-like data by having GPT-2 complete prefixes from the training set
   - Compute per-token and per-sequence perplexity for both human and synthetic data

2. **Gradient Norm Comparison** (H2)
   - Fine-tune GPT-2 on human data for N steps, recording per-step gradient L2 norms (overall + per-layer)
   - Fine-tune GPT-2 on synthetic data for N steps with identical hyperparameters
   - Compare distributions of gradient norms using statistical tests

3. **Gradient Direction Analysis** (H3)
   - Collect gradient vectors from K training steps for both human and synthetic data
   - Flatten gradients per-layer, compute PCA
   - Compare: (a) number of components needed to explain 90% variance, (b) cosine similarity between gradient directions across steps, (c) overlap between human and synthetic gradient subspaces

4. **Learning Efficiency** (H4)
   - Fine-tune GPT-2 on varying amounts of human vs synthetic data (10%, 25%, 50%, 100%)
   - Measure validation perplexity on held-out human data at each scale
   - Compare learning curves

### Baselines
- Human WikiText-2 training (gold standard)
- Random text (sanity check — should produce large, noisy gradients)

### Evaluation Metrics
- Perplexity (token-level and sequence-level) on held-out human data
- Gradient L2 norms (per-step, per-layer)
- Gradient subspace dimensionality (PCA effective rank)
- Gradient cosine similarity between human and synthetic batches
- Learning curves (val perplexity vs training steps/samples)

### Statistical Analysis Plan
- Mann-Whitney U test for gradient norm distributions (non-parametric, no normality assumption)
- Bootstrap confidence intervals for mean gradient norms
- Permutation test for gradient subspace overlap
- Significance level: α = 0.05

## Expected Outcomes
- H1: Confirmed (well-established in literature, Lin et al. 2025 shows PPL ratios of 4:1)
- H2: Synthetic gradients ~2-4x smaller in L2 norm
- H3: Synthetic gradient PCA shows ~50% fewer components for 90% variance explained
- H4: ~2-3x more synthetic samples needed for equivalent val perplexity reduction

Results *against* hypothesis would be: similar gradient norms/directions despite perplexity differences, suggesting the mechanism operates elsewhere (e.g., purely at the data distribution level without gradient-level mediation).

## Timeline and Milestones
1. Data preparation + synthetic generation: 15 min
2. Gradient norm experiment: 30 min
3. Gradient direction analysis: 30 min
4. Learning efficiency experiment: 20 min
5. Analysis and visualization: 20 min
6. Documentation: 20 min

## Potential Challenges
- GPU memory for storing full gradient vectors (mitigate: use per-layer analysis, subsample parameters)
- Synthetic data quality depends on generation strategy (mitigate: use multiple decoding strategies)
- GPT-2's relatively small size may limit generalizability (document as limitation)

## Success Criteria
- Clear, statistically significant difference in gradient norms between human and synthetic data
- Measurable difference in gradient subspace dimensionality
- Quantified learning efficiency gap with confidence intervals
- Coherent narrative linking all results to the central hypothesis
